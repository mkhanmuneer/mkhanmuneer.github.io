Loading Raw Data

The logistics, access‑logs, and dataset description files are loaded exactly as stored on disk, without any cleaning or transformation. This preserves full visibility into the original source structure before any processing.

The files use different encodings, and these choices are intentional and evidence‑based:

  • DescriptionDataCoSupplyChain.csv and DataCoSupplyChainDataset.csv contain extended ASCII characters originating from legacy export systems. Attempts to load them using UTF‑8 raise decoding errors, while Latin‑1 decodes all characters without loss or replacement. Latin‑1 is therefore the safest choice for preserving the raw text exactly as stored.

  • tokenized_access_logs.csv loads cleanly under UTF‑8 without decoding errors. The file contains URL‑encoded strings and standard ASCII/UTF‑8 characters, making UTF‑8 the correct and lossless choice.

Using the correct encoding for each file ensures that the raw datasets are ingested faithfully, without silent corruption, dropped characters, or unintended coercions. This step establishes a transparent and reproducible foundation for all downstream cleaning, mapping, and analysis.

Consolidated Inspection Summary

The inspection phase reveals that fact_access_logs_raw and fact_logistics_raw differ not only in content but in the fundamental structure of how products, categories, and customer interactions are represented. Across URL decoding, punctuation stripping, token‑overlap diagnostics, temporal distributions, and categorical cardinality checks, a consistent pattern emerges: the two datasets cannot be joined or reconciled using raw string fields alone. The mismatches are structural, systematic, and expected given the nature of the source systems.

Product Name Structure Across Datasets

Inspection of URL‑decoded paths shows that fact_access_logs_raw preserves full, human‑readable product names embedded within navigational URLs. These names include punctuation, multi‑word descriptors, model identifiers, and complete marketing language. In contrast, fact_logistics_raw contains a mixture of complete names, truncated names that end mid‑word, and generic category‑level placeholders. Punctuation‑stripped inspection confirms that truncation is widespread and systematic, with many logistics names losing key tokens required for direct alignment.

Token‑overlap diagnostics demonstrate that when the same product exists in both datasets, the token sets align strongly—even when logistics names are truncated or formatted differently. Partial overlaps reveal product‑family relationships, while zero‑overlap cases correspond to placeholders, offline‑only items, or SKUs not present in browsing logs. Token‑overlap density further shows that popular product families generate high overlap counts, while niche or generic items produce low or zero overlap. These patterns confirm that token‑level similarity is meaningful, whereas raw string equality is unreliable.

SKU Versioning and Model‑Year Differences

Several mismatches arise from SKU version differences. Logistics often contains older model years (e.g., 2014 variants), while access logs contain newer versions (e.g., 2017). These discrepancies are legitimate and reflect natural product‑lifecycle differences rather than data quality issues. They also reinforce that product identity cannot be inferred from raw names alone.

Presence of Non‑SKU and Placeholder Items

A significant subset of logistics entries represent broad categories rather than specific SKUs (e.g., “industrial consumer electronics”, “sports books”, “dvds”). These items naturally have no counterpart in access logs, which only record browsed SKUs. This explains many of the zero‑overlap cases and highlights that some mismatches reflect genuine behavioural differences rather than structural inconsistencies.

Temporal Patterns in Access Logs

Daily and hourly distributions show that access logs are heavily skewed toward a small number of high‑traffic days and peak sharply during daytime and evening hours. This confirms that browsing behaviour is bursty and time‑dependent. The temporal structure is consistent with real‑world retail traffic patterns and does not indicate data quality issues. It also suggests that any downstream behavioural modelling must account for strong temporal seasonality.

Categorical Cardinality Differences

Categorical cardinality checks reveal that fact_logistics_raw contains high‑cardinality customer attributes (e.g., thousands of unique cities, names, and streets) and a moderate number of product‑related categories (118 product names, 50 category names, 11 departments). In contrast, fact_access_logs_raw contains far fewer unique products (76), categories (33), and departments (6). This asymmetry confirms that the two datasets represent different slices of the product universe and that browsing behaviour does not cover the full transactional catalogue.

Implications for Data Integration

Across all inspection outputs, the evidence consistently shows that:

  • Raw product names cannot be used as join keys due to truncation, punctuation differences, SKU versioning, and placeholder items.
  • URL decoding and punctuation normalisation are necessary but insufficient on their own.
  • Token‑based comparison is robust to truncation and formatting differences and provides meaningful semantic signals.
  • Fuzzy matching will be required to reconcile near‑matches and truncated names.
  • Some products will never match due to behavioural differences or placeholder entries, and this is expected.

These findings justify transitioning into a structured cleaning and reconciliation phase that incorporates URL decoding, punctuation normalisation, token‑based comparison, and fuzzy matching to harmonise product identities before any cross‑dataset integration or behavioural‑to‑transactional linkage.

Dataset Grain Comparison

The two raw datasets operate at different levels of granularity:

  • fact_logistics_raw is structured at the order‑item level, where each row represents a purchased product within an order. Customer, product, and geographic attributes repeat across multiple rows.
  • fact_access_logs_raw is structured at the web‑event level, where each row represents a single product‑view or browsing action captured from the website logs.

This difference in grain is important for downstream modelling and integration. Any attempt to align behavioural activity with transactional outcomes will require careful aggregation, normalisation, and key‑mapping between these two levels.

Bronze Layer

Data Cleaning and Canonicalisation Strategy

The inspection phase establishes that the raw datasets arrive in a highly inconsistent, partially truncated, and structurally heterogeneous state. The logistics dataset is a wide, denormalised order‑item table with mixed encodings, irregular column names, masked customer fields, inconsistent date formats, and substantial missingness in key geographic attributes. The access‑log dataset, while narrower and cleaner, contains URL‑encoded paths, mixed‑casing category labels, and behavioural product names that differ structurally from the transactional product catalogue.

Manual cross‑checking against Google Maps confirms an important semantic distinction in the logistics geography fields: Customer City, Customer Country, Customer State, Customer Street, Customer Zipcode, Latitude, and Longitude describe the store location where the customer made the purchase, whereas Market, Order City, Order Country, Order Region, and Order State describe the destination region where the order is delivered or shipped. This aligns with the dataset description and means that what appear to be duplicate geographic fields are in fact two separate geographic grains: point‑of‑sale location vs. delivery region. Any cleaning, renaming, or aggregation must preserve this distinction explicitly to avoid conflating store‑level and destination‑level geographies.

Across all inspection layers — raw schema review, data types and missingness profiling, URL decoding, punctuation stripping, token‑overlap diagnostics, temporal distribution checks, and categorical cardinality analysis — a consistent pattern emerges: the two datasets cannot be reliably analysed or integrated without a structured, multi‑stage cleaning and canonicalisation process. The issues observed are not isolated anomalies but systemic characteristics of the upstream export processes.

This cleaning phase therefore focuses on establishing a unified, analysis‑ready foundation by addressing the following evidence‑based requirements:

  • Column standardisation and semantic clarification to resolve inconsistent naming conventions (spaces, underscores, parentheses, mixed casing) and to encode the confirmed semantic split between store‑location fields and delivery‑region fields in the logistics dataset.
  • Type enforcement and date parsing to convert raw text fields into proper numeric, categorical, and datetime types, ensuring temporal and numeric consistency across the pipeline.
  • Whitespace, punctuation, and encoding normalisation to correct URL‑encoded strings, trailing spaces, apostrophes, hyphens, and other formatting artefacts identified during inspection.
  • Product‑name canonicalisation informed by inspection findings: truncated logistics names, SKU‑year mismatches, placeholder items, and token‑level similarity patterns. This stage prepares the ground for later fuzzy matching and reconciliation.
  • Category and department normalisation to harmonise behavioural and transactional taxonomies, which currently differ in granularity, casing, and punctuation.
  • Missing‑value handling for sparsely populated fields such as Order Zipcode, and for structurally empty fields like Product Description.
  • Grain‑appropriate restructuring to ensure that order‑item‑level logistics data and event‑level access logs can be aggregated, mapped, and compared consistently in downstream modelling.

The goal of this phase is not to perform modelling or feature engineering, but to produce a clean, canonical, and internally consistent Silver Layer that faithfully reflects the raw data while preserving the confirmed semantic distinctions (especially in geography) and resolving the structural issues surfaced during EDA. Temporal Alignment Considerations Before Cross‑Dataset Matching

Before attempting any cross‑dataset product matching or behavioural‑to‑transactional modelling, it is essential to confirm whether the logistics and access‑log datasets operate within the same time window. Even though the Silver layer is now structurally clean and semantically consistent, the two datasets originate from different systems and may cover entirely different periods. If their temporal ranges do not overlap, then no customer in the access logs could have purchased the products recorded in the logistics dataset, and no browsing behaviour could be linked to any order.

This is a foundational requirement: cross‑dataset reconciliation is only meaningful when the datasets share a common temporal horizon.

Why Temporal Alignment Matters

  • Product matching becomes ambiguous if the access logs represent a different product catalogue period than the logistics dataset.
  • Behavioural modelling becomes invalid if browsing events occur months or years before or after the recorded purchases.
  • SKU‑level reconciliation may fail not because of naming inconsistencies, but because the products simply did not coexist in the same timeframe.
  • Customer‑level or session‑to‑order linkage becomes impossible without overlapping dates.

Given that the EDA revealed structural differences in product naming, SKU versions, and category granularity, it is especially important to rule out the possibility that these differences are driven by time‑period mismatches rather than naming inconsistencies alone.

What Needs to Be Verified

To justify any cross‑dataset integration, we must confirm:

  • the earliest and latest dates in the logistics dataset (order_date and shipping_date),
  • the earliest and latest timestamps in the access logs (date),
  • whether the two ranges overlap,
  • and if so, the size of the overlapping window.

If the datasets do not overlap in time, then:

  • product matching should not be attempted,
  • behavioural‑to‑transactional modelling is not meaningful,
  • and the two datasets should be analysed independently.

If they do overlap, then the next step is to quantify the overlap and proceed with product reconciliation and behavioural enrichment.

Next Step

Before moving forward with product matching or modelling, we should compute the temporal ranges of both datasets and verify whether they intersect. This will determine whether cross‑dataset integration is logically valid or whether the datasets should be treated as separate analytical domains.

    • tag sessions with matched logistics products where possible,

Objective: Enrich orders in logistics_overlap with behavioural context where logically valid.

    • pre‑purchase view counts,

How we compute logistics features

  • Net sales amount subtracts the applied discount from revenue and is clipped at zero.
  • Net‑sales ratio expresses the proportion of revenue retained after discounting.

These fields establish the financial backbone for downstream customer and product aggregation.

  • Actual shipping days are computed as the difference between shipping_date and order_date.

    Early, On‑time, Slight Delay, Moderate Delay, Severe Delay.

These features capture operational reliability and fulfilment performance.

  • Margin ratio is computed as
    benefit_per_order ÷ revenue,

4. Customer and product enrichment

  • High‑value order flag identifies orders whose net sales exceed twice the median order value.
  • Price buckets segment products into quantile‑based price tiers

These enrichments support segmentation, ranking, and modelling tasks across domains.

How we compute access‑log features

We operate at the page‑view grain, enriching each row with derived temporal, behavioural, and URL‑based attributes before rolling up key indicators at the session level.

1. Canonical timestamp

We standardise the raw timestamp by converting session_date_time into a canonical datetime type, ensuring consistent temporal operations across the dataset.

2. Temporal features

From the canonical timestamp, we derive: - Session year
- Session month
- Day of week
- ISO week number
- Quarter

These attributes support seasonality analysis, traffic profiling, and alignment with logistics‑side order timelines.

3. Product‑view detection

A page view is classified as a product view if its URL contains /product/.
We encode this as a binary product_view_count feature at the row level.

4. Action‑based features derived from URL patterns

Since the Silver Layer does not include an explicit action column, we infer actions directly from URL structure: - Add‑to‑cart events (/add_to_cart)
- Product page views (/product/)
- Category page views (/category/)
- Department page views (/department/)

These binary indicators allow us to reconstruct behavioural funnels and engagement patterns.

5. URL‑derived semantic features

We extract structural information from the URL: - URL depth (number of / separators)
- Last URL token, capturing the terminal semantic element (e.g., product slug, category name)

These features help differentiate between shallow navigational pages and deep product‑level interactions.

6. Canonical product naming

We retain the canonical product name when available.

7. Session‑level engagement flags and aggregates

- has_add_to_cart — whether the session contains an add‑to‑cart event
- has_key_event — alias for add‑to‑cart, used for funnel analysis
- session_page_views — total page views in the session
- session_product_views — total product views in the session

These features provide a compact behavioural summary for each session, enabling downstream modelling and cross‑domain alignment with logistics‑side customer activity.

8. Final cleanup

We remove duplicate rows and reset the index to ensure a clean, consistent Gold‑Layer dataset.

Together, these engineered features form the Gold‑Layer access‑logs table, providing a structured, high‑resolution view of customer browsing behaviour, product interaction intensity, and session‑level engagement patterns.

Session Features

We now construct our session‑level feature table, which aggregates page‑view activity into meaningful behavioural summaries aligned with modern analytics standards. This Gold‑Layer table captures engagement, bounce behaviour, duration, temporal patterns, and IP‑level signals. Below we outline the logic used to engineer these features.

How we compute session features

We begin from the enriched access‑log Gold Layer and roll up activity at the session_id level, producing a compact representation of each browsing session.

1. Key‑event detection (GA4‑aligned)

We identify key events using the is_add_to_cart flag created in the access‑log Gold Layer.
If this field is missing, we fall back to URL‑based detection (/add_to_cart).
This aligns with GA4’s emphasis on meaningful interactions rather than simple page counts.

2. Date extraction

We derive a session_date field from the timestamp to support daily aggregation and temporal alignment with logistics‑side order activity.

3. Session‑level aggregation

Using session_id, we compute:

  • Session start and session end timestamps
  • Total page views
  • Unique URL actions (unique page URLs visited)
  • Key‑event count
  • Total product views
  • IP address associated with the session

This forms the core behavioural summary for each session.

4. Duration metrics

We compute:

  • duration_seconds — time between first and last event
  • duration_minutes — normalised duration

These metrics support engagement scoring and GA4‑style session classification.

5. Universal Analytics (UA) bounce definition

For historical comparison, we compute the legacy UA bounce flag:

  • A UA bounce occurs when a session has exactly one page view.

This definition is simple but limited, as it cannot distinguish between quick exits and long single‑page reads.

6. GA4 engagement definition

We adopt the modern GA4 definition of an engaged session, which is true if any of the following hold:

  • Session lasts more than 10 seconds, or
  • Session has 2 or more page views, or
  • Session contains at least one key event

This produces a more meaningful measure of user engagement.

7. GA4 bounce definition

GA4 bounce is defined as the inverse of engagement:

  • bounce_session_ga4 = not is_engaged_session_ga4

This aligns our behavioural modelling with current industry standards.

8. Duration buckets

We segment sessions into interpretable duration categories:

  • Bounce (0–1 min)
  • Short (1–5 min)
  • Medium (5–15 min)
  • Long (15–60 min)
  • Very Long (60+ min)

These buckets support funnel analysis, segmentation, and behavioural clustering.

9. Engagement score (custom metric)

We compute a composite engagement score using a weighted blend of:

  • Page views
  • Unique URL actions
  • Key events

This produces a continuous measure of session intensity beyond binary engagement flags.

10. Temporal features

From the session start timestamp, we derive:

  • Session hour
  • Day of week

These features support hourly traffic analysis, weekday/weekend patterns, and behavioural seasonality.

11. IP frequency encoding

We compute how frequently each IP appears across all sessions.
This helps identify:

  • heavy repeat visitors
  • shared devices
  • bots or automated traffic patterns

Together, these engineered features form the Gold‑Layer session table, providing a structured, GA4‑aligned, modelling‑ready view of user engagement, browsing behaviour, and session‑level interaction patterns.

Understanding Key Events in this Dataset

Although this dataset predates Google Analytics 4 (GA4), the raw access logs contain implicit user actions embedded directly in the URL path. These actions appear as suffixes appended to product URLs, such as:

  • (potentially other actions in the full dataset)

  • 2–3 orders
  • 4–5 orders
  • 6–10 orders
  • 10+ orders

Product Features

We now compute our product‑level feature table, forming another core component of the Gold Layer. This table captures demand patterns, pricing behaviour, operational performance, and lifecycle characteristics for each product. Below we outline the logic used to derive these features.

How we compute product features

We aggregate the logistics dataset at the product_name_canonical level, producing a consolidated view of product performance across orders, customers, and time. Specifically, we compute:

1. Demand and sales metrics

  • Total number of orders containing the product (unique order_id count).
  • Total units sold across all orders.
  • Average units per order (order_item_quantity mean).
  • Total revenue generated by the product (net_sales_amount sum).
  • Average revenue per order (net_sales_amount mean).
  • Average selling price (order_item_product_price mean).
  • Average discount applied to the product.
  • Average margin ratio across all sales.
  • High‑value order rate, capturing how often the product appears in high‑value baskets.

2. Operational performance metrics

  • Average shipping duration (shipping_days_actual mean).
  • Late‑delivery rate (mean of is_delayed).
  • Average shipping delay (shipping_delay mean).

These metrics help us understand not only how well a product sells, but also how reliably it is delivered.

3. Product lifecycle metrics

  • First sold date and last sold date, establishing the product’s active selling window.
  • Product lifetime (days) computed as the difference between first and last sale.
  • Recency (days) measured as the number of days since the product was last sold relative to the dataset’s maximum sale date.

4. Segmentation buckets

To support downstream modelling, ranking, and assortment analysis, we create interpretable product‑level segments:

  • Demand buckets based on quantiles of total orders
    (Very Low → Very High).
  • Margin buckets based on quantiles of average margin ratio
    (Very Low → Very High).
